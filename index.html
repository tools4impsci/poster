Spatial Analysis of Environmental Influences on Adolescent Mental Health in Freetown, Sierra Leone

Cara M. Antonaccio, PhD, MSPH & Alethea Desrosiers, PhD, MA

Brown University, Warren Alpert Medical School, Department of Psychiatry & Human Behavior

Introduction & Background

  • In post-conflict urban settings like Freetown, Sierra Leone, key environmental factors that influence adolescent mental health are not well understood, especially their geographic patterns.
  • Understanding the spatial distribution of mental health challenges can help target interventions and plan resource allocation more effectively.
  • Objective: This study aimed to identify geographic hotspots of elevated mental health symptoms among adolescents in Freetown and to analyze the associations between these symptoms and various contextual risk factors.

Methods

  • Study Design: A spatial epidemiological analysis was conducted.
  • Data Source: Baseline data from a study on a youth-focused mental health intervention.
  • Participants: 1,200 adolescents in Freetown, Sierra Leone.
  • Measures:
    • Mental Health: Hopkins Symptom Checklist (HSCL) measured depression & anxiety symptoms.
    • Context: Socioeconomic status, population density, proximity to historical disasters/chronic environmental hazards.
  • Analysis: Thematic cartography and spatial regression models were used to examine associations.

Results

  • Analyses revealed spatial heterogeneity ("hotspots") of mental health symptoms based on school location.
  • Adolescent mental health scores were associated with school proximity to key environmental risk factors (i.e., historical landslide locations and the Freetown waste disposal site), controlling for socioeconomic status.
  • Regression results suggest that for every 100-meter increase in distance from a historical disaster site or environmental hazard, a school’s mean mental health score (HSCL) is expected to decrease by 0.01 points.
  • A stronger spatial association was observed with chronic, recurring environmental risks (like seasonal flooding) than with proximity to the site of a singular past disaster like the 2017 Regent Landslide.

Conclusions & Implications

  • The analysis indicates a relationship between the spatial distribution of historical disaster events, chronic environmental hazards, and adolescent mental health outcomes.
  • Findings support the hypothesis that chronic exposure to environmental risk and the potential for disruption may be associated with proximity to disaster locations and increase the risk for adolescents to develop symptoms of anxiety and depression.
  • Findings could help provide important information to influence government decisions about resource allocation as well as to help identify higher risk populations. For example, reducing environmental hazards could help prevent the onset of mental health conditions among adolescents in high-risk areas.
